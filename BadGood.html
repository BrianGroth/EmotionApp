Bad ↔ Good

“Bad” can describe an experience, a choice, or a feeling—but it does not have to become a verdict about who you are. This range helps you move from harsh judgment toward honest repair and appreciation.

BadGood

What this feeling may be telling you

You may be disappointed, ashamed, discouraged, or aware that something needs to change. Try separating the event from your identity: “I did something I regret” is different from “I am bad.”

Notice it in your body

  • A sinking stomach, tight throat, hot face, or a wish to hide.
  • All-or-nothing thoughts like “I always ruin things.”
  • A small pull toward hope, gratitude, responsibility, or repair.

Try this in the next five minutes

  1. 1
    Name the facts

    What happened, without adding “always,” “never,” “good,” or “bad”?

  2. 2
    Name the feeling

    Is it shame, disappointment, guilt, grief, envy, relief, or something else?

  3. 3
    Choose a repair

    Apologize, correct the mistake, ask a question, or take one action that matches your values.

If someone else feels this

“I hear that this feels really hard. Do you want comfort, help thinking it through, or a little space?”

Do not pile on with shame or rush to positive thinking. Reflect what you heard, stay curious, and help the person find one doable next step.

Complete Emotional Range

These words describe experiences and states, not permanent labels. Pick the most accurate word you can find today.

Loathing Disgust Contempt Judgmental Disappointed Neutral Acceptance Hope Optimism Satisfaction Appreciation Gratitude Inspired Uplifted

When to get more support

If shame or self-criticism keeps you stuck, affects your relationships, or makes you feel unsafe, a therapist or counselor can help you work with the feeling without being ruled by it.

Helpful places to start

  • Self-compassion and values exercises
  • Journaling or structured reflection
  • Cognitive-behavioral or counseling support

EmotionApp is a reflection and education tool, not a diagnosis or emergency service.
